Introduction Agriculture is the backbone of the Indian economy, providing livelihood to over 50% of the population and contributing significantly to GDP. Despite its critical importance, the agricultural sector faces major challenges, one of the most pressing being price volatility in agricultural produce. Farmers across the country sell their produce in mandis (wholesale markets), where prices fluctuate daily due to a combination of factors including demand-supply mismatch, weather variations, transportation constraints, and government interventions. These fluctuations often result in farmers selling their produce at unprofitable prices, leading to financial stress and discouragement. The lack of a reliable mechanism to forecast mandi prices leaves farmers in a state of uncertainty, forcing them to rely on middlemen or guesswork for deciding when and where to sell their crops. In a data-driven world, the use of technology in agriculture—commonly referred to as AgriTech—has become an effective solution to such problems. Predictive analytics and machine learning (ML) have emerged as powerful tools to address agricultural challenges by leveraging large amounts of historical and real-time data to generate actionable insights. Price prediction, in particular, has the potential to revolutionize how farmers make decisions regarding crop storage, sales timing, and transportation. By analyzing past mandi prices, seasonal trends, and external factors such as rainfall, temperature, and humidity, ML models can forecast future prices with considerable accuracy. The proposed Agri-Price Prediction System aims to bridge the information gap between farmers and the market by providing a reliable, data-driven price forecasting platform. The system will gather historical mandi price data for various crops from government portals such as Agmarknet, integrate it with real-time weather data from meteorological sources, and apply advanced ML algorithms like ARIMA, Random Forest Regression, and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to predict future prices. These models will capture both short-term and long-term price fluctuations by considering factors like seasonality, demand spikes during festivals, and production levels influenced by climatic conditions. An accurate price prediction system can empower farmers to make informed decisions on whether to sell their produce immediately after harvest or store it until prices rise. This can significantly reduce distress selling, improve profit margins, and stabilize farmer income. Moreover, such a system will benefit traders, wholesalers, and government agencies by providing early indicators of price trends, which can assist in planning procurement operations, transportation, and storage logistics. The integration of weather data further enhances prediction accuracy by correlating climatic events such as heavy rainfall, drought, or unseasonal temperature fluctuations with market price variations. This project also aligns with the broader goals of Digital India and sustainable agriculture, as it promotes the use of data-driven decision-making, minimizes post-harvest losses, and supports better supply chain management. By providing a user-friendly dashboard or mobile application interface, the system will ensure accessibility for farmers and stakeholders even in rural areas. In conclusion, the Agri-Price Prediction System represents a step towards smart farming and predictive agriculture, aiming to empower farmers with timely, actionable insights that lead to better planning, reduced financial risk, and increased profitability. As agriculture continues to modernize, such ML-driven solutions will become integral in building a resilient, sustainable, and technology-enabled agricultural ecosystem. Table 1. Literature Review Accurate forecasting of agricultural commodity prices has become a critical area of research as price volatility continues to impact farmers’ decision-making and income stability. Several studies have explored the use of statistical methods, machine learning (ML) models, and weather data integration to address this problem, forming the foundation for an effective price prediction system. Traditional Price Forecasting Approaches: Early research relied heavily on statistical models such as ARIMA (Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average) for time-series forecasting. Kumar et al. (2023) demonstrated that ARIMA models are effective in capturing seasonal price fluctuations and short-term trends in commodity markets. However, they also noted that ARIMA struggles with nonlinear patterns and fails to incorporate external variables such as climate conditions, which are crucial in agriculture. Machine Learning for Price Prediction: Recent advancements have shifted towards ML algorithms, which provide better performance in handling large, multivariate datasets. Random Forest Regression, XGBoost, and Support Vector Regression (SVR) have been successfully applied for crop price prediction, capturing complex relationships between historical prices, demand-supply data, and market signals. Sharma & Verma (2019) reported that ensemble learning techniques outperform classical statistical approaches in predicting volatile mandi prices, especially when combined with seasonal and trend features. Deep Learning Models: Deep learning has further improved forecasting accuracy. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are widely used for time-series analysis because of their ability to learn long-term dependencies. Jaiswal & Dey (2022) found that LSTM models provide superior results compared to ARIMA for long-term agricultural price prediction, making them suitable for identifying seasonal demand patterns and price peaks. Weather and External Factor Integration: Weather plays a decisive role in crop production and thus in price fluctuations. Studies by Muthusamy & Subramanian (2021) highlight the importance of integrating climate parameters such as rainfall, temperature, and humidity with price models to enhance forecasting reliability. Including such features helps in predicting production shortfalls or surpluses, which directly influence mandi prices. Decision Support and Accessibility: Beyond model accuracy, usability and farmer adoption are crucial. Chen & Zhang (2020) emphasized the development of decision support systems with intuitive interfaces, regional language support, and mobile compatibility to ensure accessibility for smallholder farmers. Such systems allow farmers to plan their sales, transportation, and storage decisions more effectively. Finally, the literature indicates a clear transition towards AI-driven and data-integrated systems for price forecasting. The proposed Agri-Price Prediction System builds upon these studies by combining historical price data, weather information, and ML-based time-series models (ARIMA, LSTM) to deliver reliable, region-specific price predictions through an easy-to-use dashboard. This approach addresses both the technical and practical challenges of price prediction in modern agriculture, ultimately supporting better decision-making and improved farmer profitability. Table 2. Problem Statement Agriculture forms the backbone of the Indian economy, with millions of farmers relying on the sale of their produce at local mandis as their primary source of income. However, crop prices in mandis are highly volatile due to varying supply-demand dynamics, changing weather conditions, and lack of transparent price forecasting mechanisms. Farmers often face uncertainty in decision-making regarding when and where to sell their produce, which frequently results in financial losses and reduced profitability. Currently, most farmers depend on guesswork, local traders, or incomplete information for price estimation, leading to unfair pricing and exploitation. Additionally, sudden weather changes, seasonal variability, and insufficient use of historical data further aggravate the problem by making price trends unpredictable. Hence, there is a need for an intelligent system that can analyze historical mandi price data and weather conditions to provide reliable predictions of future crop prices. Such a solution will empower farmers with data-driven insights, improve their bargaining power, aid in choosing the right time and market for selling produce, and ultimately contribute toward improved financial stability and sustainable agricultural practices. 5. Challenges Data Availability and Quality – Historical mandi price data is often fragmented, incomplete, or inconsistent across regions, which complicates building a reliable dataset. Unstructured Data Formats – Price records may exist in non-standard formats (PDFs, handwritten records, local systems), requiring heavy preprocessing for ML use. Weather Data Integration – Collecting accurate, location-specific weather data and aligning it with mandi-level crop price data is complex. Regional and Crop Diversity – Different crops have highly varied demand-supply dynamics, and regional variations (soil, rainfall, festivals, local events) can skew predictions. Dynamic Market Influences – Government policies, subsidies, import-export decisions, or sudden market disruptions (like pandemics) are hard to model mathematically. Non-linear and Seasonal Trends – Crop prices fluctuate seasonally and depend on many non-linear relationships that simple models may fail to capture. Model Overfitting Risk – Historical patterns may not always generalize to future unseen conditions, leading to inaccurate predictions. Interpretability of Predictions – Farmers and policymakers often need transparent explanations of why a price is predicted, which is difficult with black-box ML models. Scalability Across Regions – A model designed for one mandi or crop may not scale well to another region without retraining or adaptation. Deployment and Accessibility – Ensuring that the system is not just accurate but also practically usable by farmers (via mobile apps, low-connectivity areas, local languages) is a major challenge. 6. Motivation Agriculture is the backbone of the Indian economy, and farmers rely heavily on mandi prices for their livelihood. However, these prices are highly uncertain, influenced by factors such as supply-demand fluctuations, seasonal trends, and unpredictable weather conditions. Farmers, especially small and marginal ones, often lack access to reliable market insights and forecasting tools, forcing them to depend on middlemen who exploit the information gap. As a result, they are unable to plan the right time and place to sell their produce, leading to financial losses and reduced income security. At the same time, India generates vast amounts of agricultural and climatic data every year, but most of it remains underutilized. Advances in data science and machine learning provide an excellent opportunity to turn this raw data into meaningful predictions. By combining historical mandi price data with weather conditions, it is possible to build a robust system that forecasts future crop prices with reasonable accuracy. Such a solution can serve as a digital decision-support tool for farmers, empowering them with foresight, reducing dependency on intermediaries, and enhancing overall profitability. This motivation drives the need to develop an Agri-Price Prediction System that utilizes technology to address one of the most pressing challenges in agriculture today. 8. Proposed Methodology The methodology consists of the following modules: Data Collection – Gather historical mandi price data of major crops from government databases, market portals, and authentic repositories, along with weather data (temperature, rainfall, humidity) from meteorological sources. Data Preprocessing – Clean and standardize the collected data by handling missing values, removing outliers, and converting it into a structured format suitable for machine learning models. Feature Engineering – Identify and extract key features that influence crop prices, such as seasonal trends, demand-supply cycles, rainfall patterns, and festival/harvest periods. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) – Perform statistical and visual analysis to understand price variation trends, weather correlations, and regional disparities in crop pricing. Model Selection – Choose suitable machine learning models such as Linear Regression, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, or LSTM (for time-series forecasting) to evaluate prediction performance. Model Training – Train selected models on historical crop price and weather datasets using supervised learning techniques, ensuring price trend patterns are captured effectively. Model Validation – Split data into training/test sets and apply evaluation metrics (RMSE, MAPE, R˛ score) to measure prediction accuracy and avoid overfitting. Integration with Real-Time Data – Incorporate live weather feeds and recent mandi transactions into the system to dynamically update prediction outputs. User Interface Development – Design a simple and interactive web/mobile interface that presents price forecasts, trend graphs, and decision-support insights to farmers in an easy-to-understand format. System Deployment and Testing – Deploy the system on a scalable cloud or local server, test its real-world usability with sample data, and refine the model based on feedback. 1963420 305 0 0 Fig .1. Research established the value of machine learning in reducing risks associated with price volatility, aiding in better planning for planting, harvesting, and marketing. Conclusion The agricultural sector plays a fundamental role in the economy and food security of many countries, particularly in developing regions where a major portion of the population depends directly or indirectly on farming. However, the sector faces considerable challenges due to the inherent volatility in crop prices, influenced by numerous dynamic factors including weather conditions, market demand-supply imbalances, government policies, and logistical constraints. The unpredictability of mandi prices is a critical concern that affects farmers’ livelihoods, market efficiency, and the overall agricultural supply chain. This project, "Agri-Price Prediction System – Uses ML to predict future mandi prices of crops based on previous data history and weather condition," aims to contribute a meaningful solution to this challenge by leveraging machine learning to provide accurate and timely price forecasts. The proposed system represents a significant advancement over traditional statistical approaches, which have often fallen short in handling the nonlinear and complex nature of agricultural price data. By integrating diverse datasets—historical mandi prices combined with real-time and historical weather data—the system builds a robust predictive framework. Machine learning models, particularly those adept at capturing temporal and nonlinear trends such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks and ensemble methods, provide the backbone for this system. These models can learn intricate patterns and dependencies that human expertise and conventional methods could overlook. Data preprocessing steps such as cleaning, normalization, and feature engineering form the foundation for the model's success, enabling it to work effectively with large and potentially noisy agricultural datasets. Exploratory data analysis further clarifies the relationships between market prices and influencing factors such as rainfall, temperature, and humidity—aspects known to determine crop yield variability and quality. Consequently, the system can forecast price trends more accurately by accounting for these critical variables. The integration of machine learning into an accessible, user-friendly interface ensures that these sophisticated forecasts can benefit the end-users, primarily farmers. This interface not only visualizes predicted price trends clearly but also helps farmers make informed decisions about when to sell their harvest to optimize profits, reducing distress selling caused by lack of timely information. This empowerment is especially critical in regions where farmers have historically been vulnerable to misinformation, price exploitation, and lack of market access. Beyond individual farmers, the system offers substantial benefits to traders, government bodies, and policymakers. Traders gain insights that allow them to optimize procurement strategies, while policymakers can design interventions for price stabilization with improved market knowledge. Moreover, the forecast-driven planning aids supply chain stakeholders in logistics management and storage planning, ultimately leading to reduced post-harvest losses and better resource allocation. The project also highlights future possibilities for expansion and refinement. The modular nature of the system allows scaling to include a wider variety of crops and geographical regions. Incorporating additional factors such as government policy changes, global commodity trends, and social-economic data will further enrich the model. Continuous feedback and real-world performance monitoring enable the system to adapt and improve with new data, enhancing prediction reliability over time. One of the remarkable aspects of this work is the emphasis on sustainability. Accurate price prediction reduces unnecessary crop wastage and encourages efficient market practices, thus supporting sustainable agricultural development. By aligning crop production estimates with expected market demand, the system aids in balancing supply chains and promoting environmentally and economically sound farming practices. However, the research also acknowledges challenges. Data availability and quality remain primary concerns, requiring ongoing efforts to source comprehensive and reliable datasets. The complexity of agricultural markets, influenced by external shocks such as sudden weather extremes or policy changes, poses additional hurdles necessitating adaptive learning models. Ensuring the accessibility of the technology in rural, under-resourced areas involves overcoming infrastructural and educational constraints. Addressing these challenges forms the critical path for the system’s successful field deployment. In summary, the Agri-Price Prediction System is a vital step towards harnessing the potential of artificial intelligence and machine learning to solve real-world agricultural problems. It bridges the data-to-decision gap for stakeholders and facilitates smarter, data-backed choices. The project not only contributes technically through model innovation and integration but also socially by empowering farmers and promoting equitable market access. Looking ahead, the continuous evolution of machine learning methodologies and the increasing digitization of agriculture set a promising trajectory for this kind of system. Collaborative efforts involving government institutions, private sector players, and research bodies will be essential to scale and sustain such initiatives. Ultimately, this system embodies how technology can foster resilience and prosperity in agriculture, contributing meaningfully to food security, economic stability, and rural development globally.
